genus Berberis

Member of barberry family (family Berberidaceae)
dicots (class Magnoliopsida)
flowering plants (subphylum Angiospermae)

There are no other wild species of this genus in the bay area.

 california barberry
Berberis pinnata
california barberry
Berberis pinnata
  • compound leaves
    • usually 7–11 leaflets, ± pinnately veined
      • leaflet margin ± flat, ± thin, not hard
      • 15–23 spines per side, 0–2 mm [CalPhotos often shows fewer spines that may be > 2 mm]
  • Jepson doesn’t provide a reliable way to distinguish this reliably from oregon grape (Berberis aquifolium)
 oregon grape
Berberis aquifolium
oregon grape
Berberis aquifolium
  • compound leaves
    • usually 5–9 leaflets, ± pinnately veined
      • leaflet margin wavy, ± thin, not hard
      • 12–24 spines per side, 1–2 mm
  • Jepson doesn’t provide a reliable way to distinguish this from california barberry (Berberis pinnata)

shining netvein barberry
Berberis dictyota

  • compound leaves
    • usually 7–9 leaflets, ± pinnately veined, not shiny
      • leaflet margin strongly wavy, thick, usually hard
      • 6–10 spines per side, 2–5 mm long

cascade oregon-grape
Berberis nervosa

  • compound leaves
    • leaflets ± palmately veined

darwin’s barberry
Berberis darwinii

  • simple leaves (not opposite leaflets on a distinct stalk)

Toxicity of barberry (Berberis spp.):
4 – Ingestion of these plants, especially in large amounts, is expected to cause serious effects to the heart, liver, kidneys or brain. If ingested in any amount, call the poison center immediately.
At least one lower-level taxon has differing toxicity.
