galls on golden oaks

 ruptured twig gall wasp
Callirhytis perdens
ruptured twig gall wasp
Callirhytis perdens
  • forms within branches of red oaks (section Lobatae)
  • after the stem ruptures, the fissures turn black
    • other stem galls of red oaks don’t create black fissures
 round leaf gall wasp

round leaf gall wasp
Heteroecus flavens
  • forms within leaves of canyon live oak (Quercus chrysolepis) and huckleberry oak
 beaked spindle gall wasp
Heteroecus pacificus
beaked spindle gall wasp
Heteroecus pacificus
  • forms within the tips of branches of canyon live oak (Quercus chrysolepis) and huckleberry oak
 golden gall wasp
Heteroecus melanoderma
golden gall wasp
Heteroecus melanoderma
  • wool does not easily rub off [source]
  • forms in place of buds on branches of canyon live oak (Quercus chrysolepis) and huckleberry oak

woolly gall wasp
Heteroecus dasydactyli

  • wool is longer than on golden gall wasp (Heteroecus melanoderma)
    • but the wool rubs off easily when rubbed, leaving a smooth surface [source]
  • forms in place of buds on branches of canyon live oak (Quercus chrysolepis)

galls on white oaks

 california gall wasp
Andricus quercuscalifornicus
california gall wasp
Andricus quercuscalifornicus
  • forms on branches of white oaks (section Quercus), usually valley oak (Quercus lobata)
  • a large tan gall that looks similar to the fruit of california buckeye (Aesculus californica)
    • oak gall is attached to the side of a branch, may be on a tree with leaves, may be lumpy, has no lines on which to split
    • buckeye fruit hangs from the tip of a drooping twig, is typically seen only on leafless trees, and may be partially split in half to show a shiny brown layer beneath

red cone gall wasp
Feron kingi
  • forms on leaf surfaces of blue oak (Quercus douglasii), oregon oak (Quercus garryana), and valley oak (Quercus lobata)

Cynips douglasii

urchin gall wasp
Cynips quercusechinus
  • forms on leaf veins of white oaks (section Quercus)
  • more “spines” than spined turban gall wasp (Cynips douglasii), with little space between the spines
 clasping twig gall wasp
Disholcaspis prehensa
clasping twig gall wasp
Disholcaspis prehensa
  • constricted around middle
  • flared base generally clasps twig at least somewhat
  • cap curved and pitted
  • forms on branches of white oaks (section Quercus)
  • produces honeydew, which may attract ants

honeydew gall wasp
Disholcaspis eldoradensis

  • smooth sides
  • sits on a narrow peg-like base
  • cap is flat or barely curved and pitted
  • forms on branches of white oaks (section Quercus)
  • produces honeydew, which may attract ants
 crystalline gall wasp
Feron crystallinum

Feron crystallinum
  • forms on lower leaf surfaces of california scrub oak (Quercus berberidifolia), nuttall’s scrub oak (Quercus dumosa), blue oak (Quercus douglasii), leather oak (Quercus durata var. durata), and oregon oak (Quercus garryana)
 disc gall wasp
Feron parmula
disc gall wasp
Feron parmula
  • forms on leaf undersurfaces of white oaks (section Quercus)
 yellow wig gall wasp
Druon fullawayi
yellow wig gall wasp
Druon fullawayi

 Feron amphorus

oak leaf blister
