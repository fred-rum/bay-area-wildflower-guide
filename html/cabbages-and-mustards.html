cabbages and mustards

genus Brassica

Member of mustard family (family Brassicaceae)
flowering plants (subphylum Angiospermae)

Key to mustards

Except for old historical records and extremely rare examples that I don't expect to encounter, there are no other wild species of this genus in the bay area.

 field mustard
Brassica rapa
field mustard
Brassica rapa
  • stem 30–100 cm, simple or branched
  • basal leaves pinnately lobed, lateral lobes 2–4(6) pairs, terminal lobe obovate, wavy-dentate
  • middle and distal cauline leaves sessile, base lobed, generally clasping
  • pedicel erect
  • petals 6–11(13) mm
  • flowers much like black mustard (Brassica nigra)
  • very common
 black mustard
Brassica nigra
black mustard
Brassica nigra
  • stem 30–200 cm, generally branched distally
  • basal and lower cauline leaves pinnately lobed, serrate-dentate
  • distal cauline leaves smaller, sessile, base tapered, not lobed
  • petals 7–11(13) mm
  • flowers much like field mustard (Brassica rapa)
  • very common

brown mustard
Brassica juncea

  • stem 20–100 cm, branched distally
  • basal leaves early-diciduous, 5–100 cm, lateral lobes in 1–3 pairs
  • distal cauline leaves smaller, base tapered, not lobed
  • pedicel spreading to ascending
  • petals 9–13 mm
  • rare

cabbage
Brassica oleracea

  • stem 50–100 cm, branched distally
  • leaves fleshy
  • basal leaves pinnately lobed, 5–75 cm
  • middle and distal cauline leaves sessile, base lobed or generally clasping
  • petals 18–25 mm
  • scattered near the coast, e.g. Wilder Ranch
